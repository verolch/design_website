Mission

Improve clients' day to day operations while applying the company rebranding

Context

Tiendanube is an Argentinian Startup that empowers thousands of brands to achieve their full potential by providing them with an online store and all the tools to manage their online operation.

At the time of this project, the company had grown a lot. The service it provided had improved and bigger brands were joining as clients. But the public perception was that it was a product for people just starting to sell online, and we needed a new way to position our selves in the market.

The decision was made to rebrand the company, and an external agency was hired to make this happen. While internally, the product design team, kept working on features and delivering value to our users.

The goal

  • Position ourselves differently in the market and earn the trust of small and big business.
  • Align the design with the technical growth of our service.
  • Improve the experience of our clients everyday operations.

Team & Role

I led the design strategy and implementation, working alongside 2 designers, a product manager, a product engineer, and the QA team. While still being a hand on contributor, my main role was supporting the team through:

  • The research process
  • Defining the scope
  • Designing the UI and UX
  • Validation and Testing
  • Implementing the frontend.

Approach

Deliver an improved Admin experience, scoped within the timeline of the wider company rebranding

Research - Undestanding our users

Method: Interviews

We dived into really understanding the relationship between our users and the admin panel.Our goal was to understand:

  • How was the current navigation working for them?
  • Where could we improve?
  • What were the most used sections?
  • Were these the easiest to access?

Learnings:

We discovered that a lot of our user's pain points had to do with some features being hard to find, especially as a first-time user. But even the more seasoned users sometimes had trouble remembering how to perform everyday tasks on the platform. They even had some of our tutorials bookmarked.

Method: Card Sorting

We needed to differentiate between people who were already using the platform (as they were biased and knew where things were) and people who were using it for the first time (trials).
Based on these learnings we designed a new menu structure and tested it with customers.

Method: Quantitive data on amplitude.

We constantly checked the metrics provided by the tool to understand users click and flow.

Most clicked sub-items on the navigation.

Implementation

Paralell to the implementation of the design we worked on an adoption strategy for the new admin using an opt-in methodology

Opt-in modal with slider explaining they could go back to the old version.

Transitioning to the new structure.

At this stage, we still didn't know what the final image of the rebranding was going to look like. So our focus was on understanding how to organize the information.


Apply new branding

After the brand guide arrived and we could start applying it, we did it directly on this new structure.
We started by applying the new colours and typography.
Next, we moved on to adjusting the margins and spacing. This allowed us to align with the concepts of the brand guide: Excellence, performance, human.

The new branding applied on desktop pages.

Cutting scope

The admin has 26 sections, each with its own internal screens. Each screen has its own custom functionality with multiple states. Depending on the use case, each admin section also has many visual states. For example, lots of products, some products, empty (no products). success & error states, etc...
For a team of three people, it was a huge task. I helped the team create a strategy that would have the most impact on our goal while still being achievable.
We focused on the navigation of each one of these sections, and on the sections most used by merchants within the platform. These were the Product and Order sections.

Validation & Testing

Test with allied users & internal teams

When you make such a big change to a platform you are bound to break things. The idea was for the new brand to generate trust and demonstrate excellence. We wanted to cause a positive emotion, without interfering in users' day-to-day.

We gathered detailed feedback from allied users, the customer support team, and QA

We prioritised feedback in categories allowing us to tackle the most pressing issues before the release. We had categories such as: - UX improvements - Critical bugs - UX bugs

The Asana board where we kept track of all the feedback.

Examples of critical bugs found during this stage.

Roll-out and adoption strategy

Or as I like to call it. How to change your product while keeping your users happy.

  • Rolled out to full user base (with user facing opt-in toggle)
  • All new stores created after launch will have the new design.
  • Set goal of 90% adoption before to killing old version (V1 achieved above 50% adoption)
  • Gathered feedback from non adopters via inapp messages (whe used intercom).
  • Continuous iterations over several months (Adoption grew to above 70%)
  • Pushed to gather more feedback (remaining user pool was low engagement).
  • Decision to kill old version was made.
  • Final notice to non adopters via inapp modal.
  • Killed old version
  • 🥂

Learnings

Importance of cutting scope & focus on the bigger battles.

As long as we kept adding things and wanted to solve everything together, we extended the development time. We knew we were moving forward, but nothing was getting through to the client.

To add value we had to let go of our "designer ego" where everything must be perfect before anyone sees it, and instead start generating value for our users.

Aligning with other teams and talking things through when there is a disagreement.

We had a different understanding of what QA meant for this project. This was apparent when reviewing the bugs that were raised. This was causing frustration for the designers in my team. We got together with the QA team to solve it.

Keep asking questions.

Generating value also means taking into account that no matter how much research we have done on how to improve our admin experience, the reality is that nobody likes to change. Sometimes we will still get things wrong and that is why contant feedback and a close conection to the people using the product is so important.

Continuous improvement

After the release of the project and solving the most pressing issues, we could focus on further improvement. Helping to improve the experience and usability while leveraging our brand and identity.

My team was longing to have some more fun with the new identity too. We created new illustrations for the empty states. Redesigned Font-awesome icons into custom ones and kept customising screens and states that didn't make it into the initial release scope.
